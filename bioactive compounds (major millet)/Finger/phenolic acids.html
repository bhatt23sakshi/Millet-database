Phenolic acids

Phenolic acids are organic compounds that are derivatives of benzoic and cinnamic acids. They are commonly found in all cereals, with sorghum and millets exhibiting the widest variety of phenolic acid content. [4,5] . Phenolic acids can be classified into two main classes: hydroxybenzoic acids and hydroxycinnamic acids. Hydroxybenzoic acids are derived from benzoic acid, while hydroxycinnamic acids are derived from cinnamic acid. Finger millet is particularly rich in hydroxybenzoic acids, which make up the major free phenolic fraction of the grain. These compounds constitute about 70-71% of the phenolic acids found in finger millet. The most abundant hydroxybenzoic acids in finger millet include gallic acid, protocatechuic acid, p-hydroxybenzoic acid, vanillic acid, gentisic acid, and syringic acid. Among these, protocatechuic acid is the most prevalent free phenolic acid, with a concentration of 45 mg per 100 g of finger millet. Phenolic acids have been linked to various health benefits, including antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties, which may help prevent chronic diseases. [1,2,3] .

Conversely, the majority of the phenolic acids in finger millet are present in bound form, and they predominantly belong to the class of hydroxycinnamic acids. These include caffeic acid, chlorogenic acid, sinapic acid, cinnamic acid (trans-cinnamic acid), coumaric acid (p-coumaric acid), and ferulic acid (trans-ferulic acid). These compounds are esterified to various components of the grain, including cell wall polysaccharides and structural proteins, and are thus less bioavailable than the free phenolic acids. Nevertheless, they have been shown to exhibit a wide range of biological activities, including antioxidant, antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and anticarcinogenic properties. Ferulic acid, in particular, has been extensively studied for its potential health benefits, such as reducing blood glucose levels and improving cardiovascular health. [1,6,4,2,7,3]

Phenolic Acid Uses

Phenolic compounds are a group of phytochemicals found in cereals that have been shown to have health-promoting properties, largely due to their antioxidant activity. Among the different types of phenolic compounds, phenolic acids and flavonoids are the most abundant in whole grains. These compounds have been studied extensively due to their potential health benefits, including reducing the risk of chronic diseases such as cardiovascular disease and certain types of cancer. In addition to their antioxidant activity, phenolic compounds may also exert anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and anti-carcinogenic effects. As such, there is growing interest in the potential of these compounds as functional ingredients in food and nutraceutical products.
