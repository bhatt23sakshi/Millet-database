History

Pearl millet (Pennisetum glaucum L.) is believed to have originated in tropical western Africa around 4000 years ago. Over time, it split into two races: the globossum race, which spread to the western side, and the typhoid race, which reached eastern Africa and then migrated to India and southern Africa about 2000–3000 years ago . During the period between 3000 and 2500 BC, pearl millet rapidly spread to various African countries, driven by the increasing desiccation of the Sahara desert. While Vavilov's classic work on the origins of crops assigned pearl millet to the Ethiopian center of domestication, some evidence suggests that it may have actually originated in West Africa. For example, the wild progenitor of pearl millet (Pennisetum americanum subsp. monodii) is rare east of Sudan and has never been found in the Ethiopian highlands. Furthermore, the pearl millet found in Ethiopia lacks the necessary morphological diversity to support an Ethiopian origin. Some scholars have proposed that pearl millet was one of several West African crops domesticated by the Mande people near the headwaters of the Niger River between 4000 and 5000 BC, though this theory has been subject to considerable controversy . During this period, the headwaters of the Niger likely featured a climax tropical rainforest environment.
