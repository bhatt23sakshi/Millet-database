History

Proso millet (Panicum miliaceum) is a small cereal crop that goes by many names, including common millet, broomcorn millet, kashfi millet, hog millet, white millet, and red millet. Archaeological evidence suggests that proso millet was first domesticated before 10,000 BC in Northern China . Today, it is widely cultivated in China, India, Nepal, Russia, Ukraine, Belarus, the Middle East, Turkey, Romania, and the United States, where it is grown on approximately half a million acres each year. Growers value proso millet for its short growing season, with some varieties producing grain only 60 days after planting, and its low water requirements, which make it more efficient per unit of soil moisture than other grain species . The most popular common name for proso millet, "proso," comes from the pan-Slavic generic name for millet, which is "proso" in Croatian and "просо" (proso) in Serbian. Initially, weedy forms of proso millet were found throughout central Asia, covering a widespread area from the Caspian Sea to Mongolia, which ultimately represent the wild progenitor of proso millet from domesticated production . Proso millet is widely grown in northern China, Mongolia, Korea, Russia, Afghanistan, Pakistan, India, and southern Europe, and it was also introduced to North America .

Evolutionary history

Panicum miliaceum is a tetraploid species with a base chromosome number of 18, which is twice the base chromosome number of diploid species within the Panicum genus. It is believed that the species resulted from an allotetraploidization event, which involved a wide hybridization between two different diploid ancestors. One of the subgenomes in proso millet is thought to have been inherited from Panicum capillare or a closely related species, while the second subgenome appears to have come from an unknown diploid ancestor that is not closely related to any known diploid Panicum species. Interestingly, this second subgenome is also found in Panicum repens, a separate allotetraploid species known as torpedo grass. The two subgenomes in proso millet diverged about 5.6 million years ago, but most genes have been retained on both subgenomes, with limited amounts of fractionation. In 2019, a sequenced version of the proso millet genome was published, estimated to be approximately 920 megabase pairs in size.

Domestication and history of cultivation

Weedy forms of proso millet can be found throughout central Asia, spanning a vast area from the Caspian Sea to Xinjiang and Mongolia. It is not clear whether these weedy forms represent the wild progenitor of proso millet or feral escapes from domesticated production. Interestingly, weedy proso millet in the United States, which are thought to be feral escapes from cultivation, have become increasingly common. This suggests that current proso millet cultivars still retain the potential to revert to a weedy form, similar to what has been observed in weedy rice. The earliest archeological evidence for domesticated proso millet comes from the Cishan site in semiarid northeastern China, dating back to around 8,000 BCE. This is likely due to the fact that early varieties of proso millet had a short lifecycle, with a harvest time of as little as 45 days from planting. This made it possible for seminomadic tribes to adopt agriculture, forming a bridge between their traditional hunter-gatherer-focused lifestyles and early agricultural civilizations. Archaeological evidence for the cultivation of domesticated proso millet in East Asia and Europe can be traced back to at least 5,000 BCE, with finds in Georgia and Germany (near Leipzig, Hadersleben) by the Linear Pottery culture (Early LBK, Neolithikum 5500–4900 BCE). It is not clear whether this represents an independent domestication of the same wild ancestor, or the spread of the crop from East Asia along trade routes through the arid steppes. Evidence for cultivation in southern Europe and the Near East is comparatively more recent, with the earliest evidence for its cultivation in the Near East found in the ruins of Nimrud, Iraq, dating back to around 700 BCE.
