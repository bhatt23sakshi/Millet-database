Alkaloids

Alkaloids are natural compounds primarily found in plants, with certain families of flowering plants having a particularly high concentration of them. It is estimated that as many as one-quarter of higher plants contain alkaloids, and several thousand different types of alkaloids have been identified to date. Typically, a given species will contain only a few kinds of alkaloids, although both the opium poppy (Papaver somniferum) and the ergot fungus (Claviceps) contain about 30 different types. Plant families such as Papaveraceae (poppy family), Ranunculaceae (buttercups), Solanaceae (nightshades), and Amaryllidaceae (amaryllis) are particularly rich in alkaloids. Although rare, a few alkaloids have also been discovered in animal species such as the New World beaver (Castor canadensis) and poison-dart frogs (Phyllobates), while certain fungi, including ergot, produce them as well.

Chemical structure

Properties of Alkaloids

  • Alkaloids typically exist as colourless, crystalline solids, although some, such as coniine and nicotine, are liquid, and a few, such as berberine, are coloured (yellow).
  • While free alkaloid bases are insoluble in water, they are soluble in most organic solvents.
  • The majority of alkaloids are laevorotatory (optically active), although a few, like coniine, are dextrorotatory, and some, like papaverine, are optically inactive. Alkaloids generally have a bitter taste and possess notable physiological activity.

Application

  • In medicine

    • The use of alkaloid-containing plants for medicinal purposes has a lengthy history, and when the first alkaloids were isolated in the 19th century, they were quickly applied in clinical practice. Many synthetic and semi-synthetic drugs are modifications of alkaloids, designed to enhance or alter the primary effects of the drug while reducing undesirable side effects. For instance, naloxone, an opioid receptor antagonist used to reverse opioid overdose, is a derivative of thebaine, an alkaloid present in opium.

  • In agriculture

    • Before the advent of a broad range of synthetic pesticides with relatively low toxicity, some alkaloids, such as nicotine and anabasine salts, were utilized as insecticides. However, their application was restricted due to their high toxicity to humans.

  • Use as psychoactive drugs

    • Plants containing alkaloids and their extracts, as well as pure alkaloids, have been utilized as psychoactive substances for a long time. Some alkaloids such as cocaine, caffeine, and cathinone act as central nervous system stimulants, while mescaline and many indole alkaloids (like psilocybin, dimethyltryptamine, and ibogaine) have hallucinogenic properties. Strong narcotic painkillers like morphine and codeine are also derived from alkaloids. Additionally, some alkaloids act as precursors for semi-synthetic psychoactive drugs, like ephedrine and pseudoephedrine, which are used to produce methcathinone and methamphetamine, and thebaine, which is utilized in the synthesis of many painkillers such as oxycodone.
