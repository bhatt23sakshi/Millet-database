Phytochemicals

Phytochemicals are biologically active chemical compounds found naturally in different components of plants, such as roots, stems, leaves, flowers, fruits, and seeds, that provide therapeutic and nutritional benefits for humans [1, 2]. These chemicals guard plants from diseases and environmental threats, such as pollution, stress, drought, UV exposure, and pathogenic attack, while also contributing to their color, aroma, and flavor, and are known as phytochemicals [3, 4]. Recently, evidence has gained prominence suggesting that consuming phytochemicals in large quantities can play a role in protecting human health [5, 6]. Fruits such as tomatoes, grapes, cherries, strawberries, and raspberries; vegetables such as broccoli, cabbage, carrots, onions, and garlic; legumes; whole grains and millets; nuts; seeds; fungi; and herbs and spices are common sources of phytochemicals [4, 7]. These phytochemical compounds exhibit biological characteristics such as antioxidant activity, antimicrobial property, modulation of detoxifying enzymes, stimulating immune system, a reduction in platelet aggregation and modulating hormone metabolism, and anticancer activity [8]. Phytochemicals are classified as primary or secondary metabolites based on their portrayal in plant metabolism. Primary metabolites include the common sugars, amino acids, proteins, purines, and pyrimidines of nucleic acids, and chlorophyll. Secondary metabolites constitute alkaloids, terpenes, flavonoids, lignans, plant steroids, curcumins, saponins, phenolics, and glucosides [9]. Most phytochemicals possess numerous properties that are important for plants and animals. These properties, such as antioxidant activity, guard the cells from oxidative damage and reduce the possibility of developing certain kinds of diseases like cancer. Phytochemicals with antioxidant activities include carotenoids (found in fruits), flavonoids (found in fruits and vegetables), and polyphenols (tea, grapes) [10]. The antimicrobial properties are prominent with phytochemicals like allicin

Health Benefits of Phytochemicals

Information on the health-promoting potential of vegetables and fruits as well as on isolated phytochemicals has been obtained from different experimental systems (cell and tissue cultures, animal experiments, human studies). Of particular relevance are human studies, especially epidemiological and intervention studies, and biomarker-related studies.

  • Cardiovascular Diseases (CVD)

    • Cardiovascular diseases (CVD) remain the leading cause of death globally, but adopting healthy lifestyle choices can prevent up to 90% of cases. Such choices include regular physical activity, avoiding tobacco smoke, limiting alcohol intake, and following a healthy diet. Additionally, managing high blood pressure and diabetes can also decrease the risk of developing CVD. While CVD primarily affects older adults, men tend to experience disease onset seven to ten years earlier than women. A systematic review showed that consuming six classes of flavonoids - flavonols, anthocyanidins, proanthocyanidins, flavones, flavanones, and flavan-3-ols - significantly decreases the risk of developing CVD. Studies involving older women indicated that high consumption of flavonoid-rich foods, including tea, chocolate, red wine, fruits, and vegetables, may lower the risk of all-cause mortality, cancer mortality, and CVD mortality. Furthermore, meta-analyses demonstrated that elevated flavonoids intake can have a protective effect on coronary heart disease (CHD) and stroke. The findings suggest that increasing the consumption of flavonoid-rich foods can reduce the risk of developing CVD and its associated complications.

  • Cancer

    • Cancer is a leading cause of death in developed countries, and diet is a major factor that can influence cancer risk. A diet rich in vegetables and fruits has been linked to a lower risk of various types of cancer, as these foods contain phytochemicals that exhibit anti-cancer properties. Experts recommend consuming at least five servings of fruits and vegetables per day, limiting processed and red meat intake, avoiding excessive alcohol consumption, and maintaining a healthy weight, not smoking, and minimizing sun exposure to reduce the risk of many cancers.

  • Diabetes

    • Diabetes is a global health issue, with an estimated 400 million people affected worldwide, of which type 2 diabetes accounts for approximately 90% of cases. Both men and women are equally affected, representing 8.3% of the adult population. The prevention and management of type 2 diabetes involves adopting a healthy diet, maintaining a normal body weight, engaging in regular physical exercise, avoiding tobacco use, controlling blood pressure, and proper foot care. Those with type 1 diabetes require insulin injections for management. Diabetes doubles the risk of mortality.

  • Osteoporosis

    • Osteoporosis is a common condition affecting a significant proportion of the population, particularly women and the elderly. Depending on the diagnostic method used, 2-8% of men and 9-38% of women in developed countries may be affected. The prevalence of osteoporosis increases with age, with around 15% of white people in their 50s and up to 70% of those over 80 years of age affected. Prevention of osteoporosis should begin early in life with a proper diet during childhood, and efforts to prevent broken bones in those with osteoporosis should focus on good nutrition, exercise, and fall prevention. Lifestyle changes, such as quitting smoking and reducing alcohol consumption, can also help.

  • Cataract

    • Cataracts are a major cause of blindness, affecting approximately 20 million people worldwide. The risk of developing cataracts increases with age, but there are steps individuals can take to help prevent them, such as wearing sunglasses, avoiding smoking, and consuming foods rich in lutein, such as kale, spinach, and egg yolk. A recent meta-analysis investigated the relationship between blood lutein and zeaxanthin concentrations and the risk of cataracts. The study found a significant inverse association between these nutrients and nuclear cataracts, with pooled relative risks ranging from 0.63 for zeaxanthin to 0.73 for lutein. Additionally, high blood levels of lutein and zeaxanthin were associated with a reduced risk of cortical and subcapsular cataracts. While these findings suggest that consuming foods high in lutein and zeaxanthin may help prevent cataracts, no significant associations were found for age-related cataract in other regions of the lens. Further research is needed to fully understand the relationship between these nutrients and cataract development in different parts of the lens. Overall, maintaining a healthy diet and lifestyle, including regular consumption of lutein-rich foods, may help reduce the risk of cataracts, particularly nuclear cataracts. However, individuals should consult with their healthcare provider for personalized recommendations regarding cataract prevention and treatment.
