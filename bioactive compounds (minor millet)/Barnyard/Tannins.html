Tannins

Tannins are a diverse group of phenolic compounds that have the ability to precipitate proteins. They are composed of a wide range of oligomers and polymers and can form complexes with proteins, starch, cellulose, and minerals. Tannins are generally soluble in water, except for some high molecular weight structures. There are two main categories of tannins: hydrolyzable tannins, which include gallotannins, ellagitannin complex tannins, and proanthocyanidins; and condensed tannins. Tannins can be found in both gymnosperms and angiosperms, and have been reported in 180 families of dicotyledons and 44 families of monocotyledons. Some well-known families of species containing tannins include Aceraceae, Actinidiaceae, Anacardiaceae, Bixaceae, Burseraceae, Combretaceae, Dipterocarpaceae, Ericaceae, Grossulariaceae, and Myricaceae for dicotyledons, as well as Najadaceae and Typhaceae for monocotyledons. In the oak family Fagaceae, 73% of the species tested were found to contain tannins.

Chemical structure

IUPAC Name

[2,3-dihydroxy-5-[[(2R,3R,4S,5R,6S)-3,4,5,6-tetrakis[[3,4-dihydroxy-5-(3,4,5-trihydroxybenzoyl)oxybenzoyl]oxy]oxan-2-yl]methoxycarbonyl]phenyl] 3,4,5-trihydroxybenzoate

Molecular Formula

C76H52O46

Properties of Tannins

  • Tannins are soluble in water, alcohol, glycerol, and acetone as well as dilute alkalis.
  • However, they are only sparingly soluble in chloroform, ethyl acetate, and other organic solvents.
  • Tannins are known for their astringent taste.
  • When combined with iron compounds, they can yield a purple, violet, or black precipitate.
  • Additionally, when tannins combine with alkaloids, they form tannates, most of which are insoluble in water.

Health benefits of Tannins

  • Accelerate blood clotting
  • Reduce blood pressure
  • Decrease the serum lipid level
  • Produce liver necrosis
  • Modulate immunoresponses.
