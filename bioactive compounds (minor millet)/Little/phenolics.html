Phenolics

Phenolic compounds are a diverse group of organic molecules characterized by an aromatic ring with one or more hydroxyl groups and various substituents. In plants, phenolic compounds can be classified into several subgroups including flavonoids, phenolic acids, lignans, and phytoestrogens (isoflavones). Phenolic acids derive from benzoic acid (C6-C1 structure) and include p-hydroxybenzoic, gallic, syringic, vanillic, and protocatechuic acid, as well as other derivatives of cinnamic acid (C6-C3 structure) such as caffeic, ferulic, sinapic, and p-coumaric acids. Flavonoids are characterized by a basic C6-C3-C6 structure and include anthocyanin pigments, flavonols, flavanols, and isoflavones [1]. These compounds have been widely recognized for their potential health benefits. On the other hand, tannins are classified as condensed or hydrolysable phenolic compounds that act as antinutrients by binding proteins to form soluble and insoluble complexes, thereby inhibiting protein digestion. Tannins are found in cereals and are particularly effective at binding to proteins rich in imino acid proline groups [1].

Phenolic compounds, which are found throughout the plant kingdom, are important sources of antioxidants in millets. These compounds possess antioxidant properties that play a crucial role in maintaining the body's oxidative balance and providing protection from oxidants, oxidative processes, and reactive species. Little millet cultivars have been found to have higher concentrations of phenolics compared to foxtail millet varieties, with concentrations varying depending on the fractions obtained after milling of grains. Little millets are also rich in bioactive compounds such as gallic acid, vanillic acid, p-hydroxybenzoic acid, sinapic acid, chlorogenic acid, caffeic acid, ferulic acid, and p-coumaric acid. Dietary phenolics are classified into three major groups: flavonoids, phenolic acids, and polyphenols. Polyphenols, which are micronutrients obtained from plant-based foods, are particularly rich in antioxidants and have potential health benefits such as improving digestion, managing weight, and reducing the risk of various diseases such as cancer, osteoporosis, neurodegenerative disease, and cardiovascular diseases. Examples of foods rich in polyphenols include quinoa, flaxseed, and hempseed. However, it's worth noting that water-soluble phenol and peroxidase can be responsible for the off-odor of millet flour during storage. Despite this, the existence of phenolics with antioxidant properties supports plant-based bioactivity in the administration or curing of various diseases through therapeutic synergism.

Health benefits

Phenolic acids are a group of compounds that can be easily absorbed by the walls of your intestinal tract. Consuming these compounds may offer various health benefits, including antioxidant properties that can prevent cellular damage caused by free radicals. Additionally, regular consumption of phenolic acids may help promote anti-inflammatory conditions in your body. [14]. Drinking coffee has been linked to a reduced risk of certain cancers and chronic diseases, possibly due to its content of caffeic acid and chlorogenic acid, as well as other potentially beneficial compounds. On the other hand, red wine also contains phenolic acids, along with resveratrol, another polyphenol that may have positive health effects. Therefore, both coffee and red wine can be valuable sources of these beneficial compounds.[16] .
